About the Underground Cemetery

The Nagcarlan Underground Cemetery is a historic cemetery located in Nagcarlan, Laguna, Philippines. Built in 1845 under the supervision of Franciscan priest Fr. Vicente Velloc, it served as a burial place for Spanish friars, prominent citizens, and elite Catholic families during the Spanish colonial era.

What makes this cemetery unique is its underground crypt that lies beneath the chapel, the only one of its kind in the Philippines. The main entrance to the cemetery features a large stone arch with iron gates, displaying remarkable baroque architecture.

Historical Significance

During the Philippine Revolution against Spain in the late 19th century, the underground cemetery served as a secret meeting place for Filipino revolutionaries. It was here that plans were made and revolutionary documents were discreetly exchanged.

The cemetery was declared a National Historical Landmark by the National Historical Commission of the Philippines in 1973, recognizing its cultural and historical importance to the country.

Architecture

The cemetery showcases a blend of Spanish colonial and Filipino architecture. The main structure consists of a circular cemetery with a chapel at its entrance. The walls are made of red bricks and volcanic tuff, typical of Spanish colonial architecture in the Philippines.

The underground crypt, accessible via a flight of stairs, contains 36 niches where prominent Spanish friars and elite Filipinos were buried. The above-ground section contains 240 niches arranged in neat rows along the circular wall.

Visiting Tips

  • Best time to visit: Early morning or late afternoon to avoid the heat and crowds
  • Photography: Permitted in most areas, but flash photography is prohibited inside the crypt
  • What to wear: Comfortable walking shoes and modest attire as this is a religious site
  • Etiquette: Maintain a respectful demeanor as this is both a historical site and a burial ground
  • Guided tours: Available upon request at the entrance
